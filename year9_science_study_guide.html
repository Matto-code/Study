2 & 3. Types of neurons and their roles

Sensory neuron
Carries signals TO the CNS from receptors (skin, eyes, ears). Detects the stimulus.
Relay neuron
Found inside the CNS. Connects sensory and motor neurons. Processes and routes signals.
Motor neuron
Carries signals FROM the CNS to effectors (muscles and glands). Causes the response.

Memory trick: Sensory = to the brain  |  Motor = Muscle action

5. Neurotransmitters

When an impulse reaches the synapse, it cannot jump the gap electrically. Instead neurotransmitters carry the signal chemically:

Step 1: Impulse arrives at axon terminal
Step 2: Vesicles release neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft (gap)
Step 3: Neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the next neuron's dendrites
Step 4: A new electrical impulse is triggered in the next neuron

Examples: Acetylcholine Dopamine Serotonin Adrenaline

6. CNS vs PNS

Central Nervous System (CNS)
Brain + spinal cord. The command centre — receives, processes and coordinates all signals.
Peripheral Nervous System (PNS)
All nerves outside the brain and spinal cord. Carries signals to and from the CNS.

Memory trick: CNS = Centre of the body | PNS = Periphery (everything else)

7. Autonomic vs Somatic (divisions of the PNS)

Somatic
Voluntary control. Controls skeletal muscles — things you consciously choose to do (walking, writing).
Autonomic
Involuntary control. Regulates automatic functions: heart rate, digestion, breathing, pupil size.

The autonomic system has two branches: Sympathetic (fight-or-flight — speeds up) and Parasympathetic (rest-and-digest — slows down).

8. Parts of the CNS

Brain — main control centre, divided into regions with different functions (see Brain tab).
Spinal cord — bundle of nerves down the spine. Relays signals between brain and body; processes reflex arcs.
Meninges — three protective membranes surrounding the brain and spinal cord.
Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) — cushions and protects the CNS from impact.

10. Which brain part controls what?

Heart rate and breathing — Brainstem (medulla oblongata). Completely automatic.
Memory — Cerebrum (hippocampus in the limbic system). Short and long-term.
Emotions — Cerebrum / limbic system (amygdala). Fear, happiness, anger.
Eyes / vision — Occipital lobe of the cerebrum (back of head). Interprets visual signals.
Growth — Pituitary gland releases growth hormone (GH), controlled by the hypothalamus.
Controlling the endocrine system — Hypothalamus signals the pituitary gland, which signals other glands (the HPA axis).

Key exam point: the hypothalamus is the crucial link between the nervous system and the endocrine system — it receives nerve signals and responds with hormones.

12. Define homeostasis

Homeostasis is the process by which the body maintains a stable internal environment despite changes in external or internal conditions.

The body monitors variables (temperature, blood glucose, water) and uses feedback loops to keep them at a set point.

16. Negative feedback loops

Negative feedback detects a change from the set point and triggers a response that opposes and reverses that change.

Receptor
detects change
→
Command centre
compares to set point
→
Effector
corrects the change
↩ Output detected → response reduces → system stabilises (negative feedback)

It is called negative because the response goes in the opposite direction to the original change.

13. How the body controls blood glucose

Interactive: blood glucose simulator
None
None

If glucose rises (after eating)

Receptor: Beta cells in pancreas detect high glucose
Command centre: Pancreas releases insulin
Effector: Cells absorb glucose; liver stores it as glycogen → glucose falls ✓

If glucose falls (exercise/fasting)

Receptor: Alpha cells in pancreas detect low glucose
Command centre: Pancreas releases glucagon
Effector: Liver converts glycogen back to glucose → glucose rises ✓
Diabetes: In Type 1 diabetes, beta cells are destroyed — no insulin is produced. The feedback loop is broken. Insulin injections manually replace the missing signal.

14. How the body controls temperature

Interactive: temperature regulation simulator
20°C
None

If too cold

Receptor: Thermoreceptors in skin + hypothalamus
Command centre: Hypothalamus
Effectors: Shivering (heat from muscles), vasoconstriction (narrow blood vessels to reduce heat loss), goosebumps (trap air insulation)

If too hot

Receptor: Thermoreceptors detect temperature rise
Command centre: Hypothalamus
Effectors: Sweating (evaporative cooling), vasodilation (widen blood vessels to release heat at skin)

15. How the body controls water levels (osmoregulation)

If dehydrated

Receptor: Osmoreceptors in hypothalamus detect concentrated blood
Command centre: Hypothalamus signals pituitary gland
Effector: Pituitary releases ADH → kidneys reabsorb more water → less urine, more concentrated → water restored ✓

If overhydrated

Receptor: Osmoreceptors detect dilute blood
Command centre: Hypothalamus reduces ADH signal
Effector: Less ADH → kidneys excrete more water → more dilute urine → water restored ✓

ADH = Anti-Diuretic Hormone. Anti-diuretic = prevents urination. More ADH = less urine = body retains water.

17. Endotherm vs Ectotherm — definitions

Endotherm (warm-blooded)

Generates body heat internally through metabolism. Maintains a constant body temperature regardless of the environment. Relies heavily on negative feedback.

Examples: Mammals (humans, dogs, whales) and birds

Ectotherm (cold-blooded)

Relies on external heat sources (sun, environment) to regulate body temperature. Body temperature varies with the surroundings.

Examples: Reptiles (lizards, snakes), fish, amphibians, insects

Memory trick: Endo = Internal heat | Ecto = External heat

18. Analysing graphs — endo or ectotherm?

On a graph of body temperature vs environmental temperature:

Endotherm: Body temperature stays roughly flat/horizontal as external temperature changes — the body compensates via feedback mechanisms.
Ectotherm: Body temperature follows a diagonal line matching environmental temperature — it rises and falls with the environment.
Graph pattern identifier — click to check your understanding
Endotherm
High, constant energy use. Active in cold. No warm-up needed. Warm fur/feathers for insulation.
Ectotherm
Low energy use — very efficient. Slower in cold. Must bask to warm up. Often hibernates in winter.

Sample exam answer template

"The data shows animal [X] is an [endotherm/ectotherm] because its body temperature [remained stable at ~37°C / changed proportionally with the environment]. At an external temperature of [X°C], the body temperature was [Y°C]. [Endotherms use negative feedback via the hypothalamus to maintain a constant internal temperature / Ectotherms rely on external heat sources and behavioural thermoregulation such as basking in the sun.]"

19. Analysing data tables

Step 1 — Read the headings. What variables are measured? What are the units?
Step 2 — Identify the pattern. Does one variable increase as the other increases (positive correlation)? Decrease (negative)? No pattern?
Step 3 — Find any anomaly. Is there a data point that doesn't fit the pattern? Note it — don't ignore it.
Step 4 — Link to biology. Why does this pattern make biological sense? Reference the relevant feedback system.

Exam tip: always use specific numbers. E.g. "As temperature increased from 20°C to 40°C, heart rate increased from 65 to 95 bpm — an increase of 30 bpm."

19–20. Graph analysis practice

Flat body temp despite changing external temp → Endotherm. Write: "Body temperature remained constant at approximately X°C despite environmental changes, indicating internal regulation via negative feedback through the hypothalamus."
Body temp rises/falls with external temp → Ectotherm. Write: "Body temperature increased proportionally with environmental temperature, indicating the animal relies on external heat sources and lacks internal thermoregulatory mechanisms."
Comparing nervous vs endocrine data: If a response is immediate (within milliseconds) → nervous system. If delayed but prolonged → endocrine system.
