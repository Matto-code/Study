Landscape vs Landform

Landscape
A large area of land with distinct visual features that make it look different from other areas. It's the "big picture" of an area.

Examples: desert, rainforest, coast, mountain range, farmland
Landform
A natural geographical feature or shape on the Earth's surface. It's a specific feature within a landscape.

Examples: mountain, sand dune, river, valley, cliff, reef
A desert is a landscape. A sand dune inside that desert is a landform. The landscape is the big picture; the landform is a specific feature within it.

Weathering & erosion

Weathering
The breaking down of rock in place (it doesn't move). Caused by water, wind, ice, temperature changes, plants and chemicals.
Erosion
The wearing away and transporting of soil and rock from one place to another. Caused by water, wind, ice, gravity. Waves crashing against cliffs is coastal erosion.
Weathering breaks rock apart where it sits. Erosion moves the broken pieces somewhere else. Both reshape landforms over time.

Layers of the Earth

Core (innermost)
Made of iron and nickel. Has a solid inner core and liquid outer core. Extremely hot (~5000°C). Generates Earth's magnetic field.
Mantle (middle)
Thickest layer. Made of semi-solid (semi-fluid) silicate rock. Convection currents in the mantle are what move the tectonic plates. The upper mantle is part of the lithosphere.
Crust (outermost)
The thinnest layer — Earth's outer shell. Two types: continental crust (thick, lighter, forms land) and oceanic crust (thin, denser, under oceans).
Lithosphere
The crust + the upper part of the mantle together. The lithosphere is broken into tectonic plates that float on the semi-fluid mantle below.
Convection currents are the engine: heat from the core causes the semi-fluid mantle to circulate like boiling water. This movement drags the tectonic plates along.

What are tectonic plates?

The Earth's surface (lithosphere) is broken into large pieces called tectonic plates that fit together like a giant jigsaw puzzle. These plates float on the semi-fluid mantle and move slowly due to convection currents — heat from the core creates circular currents in the mantle that drag the plates along.

4 types of plate boundaries

Where plates meet = plate boundaries. Most earthquakes, volcanoes and mountain-building happen at these boundaries.
1. Convergent — Subduction
Plates move toward each other. An oceanic plate dives under a continental plate (because it's denser). This is called subduction. The subducting plate melts → magma rises → volcanic activity, deep ocean trenches, island arcs, and strong earthquakes.
2. Convergent — Collision
Two continental plates collide. Neither can subduct (both too thick/buoyant). They crumple and fold upward → mountain ranges (e.g. the Himalayas formed from India colliding with Asia). Also causes earthquakes.
3. Divergent
Plates move apart. Magma rises from the mantle to fill the gap → creates new oceanic crust, mid-ocean ridges, and seafloor spreading. Can cause earthquakes and volcanic activity.
4. Transform (Conservative)
Plates slide past each other horizontally. No crust is created or destroyed. Stress builds up → sudden release → violent earthquakes (e.g. San Andreas Fault). No volcanic activity.

The Ring of Fire

The Ring of Fire is the area around the Pacific Ocean where many tectonic plates meet. This zone has ~75% of the world's volcanoes and ~90% of earthquakes. It forms a horseshoe shape around the Pacific, passing through New Zealand, Indonesia, Japan, Alaska, and the west coasts of the Americas.

Earthquakes

Earthquakes occur when stress builds up at plate boundaries as plates push against, pull apart, or slide past each other. When the stress is released suddenly, the ground shakes.
Focus
Where the earthquake originates — the point underground where rocks first break/slip.
Epicentre
The point on the Earth's surface directly above the focus. This is where shaking is usually strongest.
Focus = underground origin. Epicentre = surface point directly above it. The epicentre is NOT the origin — it's the surface projection.
Where do large earthquakes occur? At transform and convergent boundaries — but earthquakes can happen anywhere plates interact.

Volcanoes

Volcanoes form when magma from the mantle rises through cracks in the crust and erupts on the surface. Three main ways:
Subduction zones: Oceanic plate subducts → melts → magma rises → erupts as volcano.
Mid-ocean ridges (divergent): Plates move apart → magma rises to fill the gap → creates underwater volcanoes and new crust.
Hotspots: Stationary heat sources in the mantle create plumes of magma that can punch through the plate above (e.g. Hawaiian islands).

Tsunamis

Tsunamis are caused by the sudden vertical displacement of a large volume of water, usually from an undersea earthquake at a subduction zone. When the ocean floor shifts, it pushes the water above it upward, generating enormous waves that travel across the ocean.

What is a biome?

A biome is a major ecological community that includes ecosystems with similar climates and organisms. Examples: rainforest, desert, grassland, tundra.

The 4 layers of the rainforest (top → bottom)

1. Emergent layer (top)
The tallest trees that poke above the canopy. Full sunlight. Wildlife: birds, bats, butterflies.
2. Canopy layer
A thick, continuous "umbrella" of treetops. Gets most sunlight. Blocks light and intercepts rainfall for layers below. Keeps lower layers humid. Wildlife: birds, snakes, sloths, monkeys.
3. Understory
Below the canopy. Dim light, warm, humid. Smaller trees, shrubs, young trees growing toward the canopy. Wildlife: frogs, insects, snakes, jaguars.
4. Forest floor (bottom)
Very little sunlight — only 2–3% reaches here. Dark, damp. Decomposing leaves and fallen trees. Wildlife: insects, snakes, lizards, paddymelons.
The canopy is the key layer — it blocks sunlight, intercepts rainfall, and keeps the lower layers consistently humid. This is why the forest floor is so dark.

Epiphytes

Epiphytes are plants that grow on other plants (usually trees) without taking nutrients from their host. They are NOT parasites. They get water and nutrients from the air, rain, and debris that collects around them. Orchids and ferns are common epiphytes.

Causes of deforestation

Agriculture (farming) — the main cause. Clearing land for crops and livestock, e.g. palm oil plantations.
Urbanisation — building housing and infrastructure for growing populations.
Mining — extracting resources from beneath the forest.
Logging — harvesting wood, paper, and other forest resources.
Who poses the most threat to rainforests? Humans.

Management strategies

1. Protected areas

What: Limit access to and use of rainforest areas to preserve them.
Disadvantages:
• People are limited in their ability to earn money from resources — can increase poverty
• Expensive to maintain and monitor — boardwalks, pathways, monitoring visitors

2. Sustainable use

Eliminating use of original rainforest: Avoiding cutting down old-growth forest that has never been logged. Use forest plantations instead to preserve endangered species habitats.
Mixed use agriculture and forestry (alley cropping): Planting crops among alleyways of trees — using forests for food production in a sustainable way without destroying the forest.
Sustainable use reduces negative impacts on rainforests. By using plantations instead of original forest, endangered species habitats can be preserved.

BOLTSS — the 6 map conventions

Every proper map should include all 6 of these. If you're asked "what conventions are missing?" — check each one.
B — Border: a frame around the map
O — Orientation: a compass rose or north arrow
L — Legend: a key explaining symbols used
T — Title: what the map shows
S — Scale: shows the ratio of map distance to real distance
S — Source: where the map data came from

Compass directions

8 compass points: N, NE, E, SE, S, SW, W, NW

When asked "which direction is X FROM Y" — stand at Y and point toward X. The direction you point is the answer.
"Which direction is Martindale FROM Place Fell?" → Stand at Place Fell, point to Martindale → answer: North East.

Grid references

4-figure grid reference

Along the corridor, up the stairs.
1. Read the easting (horizontal number) first — go ALONG the bottom
2. Then read the northing (vertical number) — go UP the side

Example: 0379 means easting 03, northing 79. The reference identifies a whole grid square.

6-figure grid reference

More precise — pinpoints a location within a grid square.
1. Read the easting (2 digits) then estimate a 3rd digit (0–9) for how far across the square
2. Read the northing (2 digits) then estimate a 3rd digit (0–9) for how far up the square

Example: 035792 means easting 03.5, northing 79.2 — a specific point within the square.
Always remember: along the corridor first (eastings), then up the stairs (northings). Eastings BEFORE northings.

Scale and distance

Maps use a scale to show how map distances relate to real distances. Use a ruler or paper edge to measure on the map, then compare to the scale bar to convert to real distance (km or m).

What are contour lines?

Contour lines are lines on a map that connect points of equal elevation (height). They show the shape of the land — whether it's flat, hilly, steep, or gentle.
Contour interval = the difference in height between each contour line. If the interval is 100 m, each line represents a 100 m increase in elevation.

Reading contour lines

Lines close together
= Steep slope
Big elevation change over a short distance
Lines far apart
= Gentle slope
Gradual elevation change over a larger distance
Spot height — an exact elevation marked with a dot and number on the map (e.g. ▲ 501m). More precise than reading contour lines.
When asked for the "highest possible point" — it's the next contour line value minus 0.1. For example, if the highest contour is 14000 m and the interval is 1000 m, the highest possible point is 14,999 m (just below the next line that doesn't exist on the map).

Key contour skills for the exam

Finding elevation of a point: Find which two contour lines the point sits between, then estimate.
Calculating total elevation change: If walking from point A (200 m) to point B (500 m), the total change = 500 − 200 = 300 m uphill.
Identifying steep vs gentle: Circle areas where lines are packed tightly (steep) or spread wide apart (gentle).
Direction of river flow: Rivers flow from high to low elevation — the V-shapes in contour lines point upstream (uphill).
Best place to build a house: Choose flat ground (lines far apart), near water, and not on a steep slope or flood zone.
